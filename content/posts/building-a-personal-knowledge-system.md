The best knowledge system is not the one with the most features. It is the
one you can still understand—and still trust—a year from now.

Most systems fail because they optimize for capture. Saving a link is easy;
recovering the idea that made it valuable is hard. A durable system needs to
make retrieval and reflection as natural as collection.

## Start with three kinds of notes

I use three simple categories:

1. **Scratch notes** hold unfinished thoughts and fleeting observations.
2. **Reference notes** summarize something learned from a source.
3. **Evergreen notes** explain one idea in my own words and evolve over time.

This is enough structure to make a note’s purpose obvious without requiring
a complicated filing system.

## Write for your future self

A useful note answers three questions:

- What is the idea?
- Why did it matter when I saved it?
- Where might I use it?

Context is more valuable than volume. Two sentences written today can save
twenty minutes of reconstruction six months from now.

## Prefer links over folders

Folders ask an idea to live in one place. Links allow it to participate in
several lines of thought. A note about feedback loops might belong equally to
software design, learning, and organizational behavior.

The goal is not to build a perfect map. It is to leave enough paths that the
important ideas can be found again.

## Keep the maintenance budget small

Set a hard limit on organizational work. Ten quiet minutes at the end of the
day is enough to rename unclear notes, add a useful link, and turn one scratch
note into something durable.

The system should support the work. It should never become the work.
